01 — Introduction

Toyota Gazoo Racing (TGR)

Toyota Gazoo Racing (TGR) is the global motorsport and high-performance division of Toyota Motor Corporation. The brand plays a central role in Toyota's performance positioning, competing in elite competitions such as the 24 Hours of Le Mans and the World Rally Championship. Beyond racing, TGR contributes to the development of performance-oriented road cars such as the GR Yaris and GR Supra.

In the digital era, social media platforms particularly Instagram have become essential tools for automotive brands to engage audiences. Instagram's visual-first interface makes it particularly suitable for motorsport brands, enabling them to communicate speed, design, and excitement effectively.

This report evaluates how Toyota Gazoo Racing uses Instagram to engage consumers through the Ladder of Engagement framework (Chaffey and Smith, 2022) and critically assesses the platform using digital marketing theory. The analysis demonstrates how TGR transforms passive viewers into active brand advocates while identifying areas for improvement.

02 — The Ladder of Engagement

Chaffey & Smith's Digital Marketing Framework
Figure 1. The ladder of engagement illustration (Stronks, 2021)

The Ladder of Engagement is a digital marketing framework that illustrates how consumers interact with brand content at progressively deeper levels, from passive observation to active contribution and collaboration. Unlike traditional models that focus on purchase or loyalty, this framework emphasises behavioural engagement in online environments, particularly on platforms such as Instagram where interaction is visible and measurable (Chaffey and Smith, 2022; Tuten and Solomon, 2020).

At its core, the model recognises that not all users engage equally. Most remain passive, while a smaller proportion contribute through likes, comments, sharing, or content creation. This distribution aligns with the participation inequality principle proposed by Jakob Nielsen (2006), which suggests a 90:9:1 pattern of engagement. For marketers, the objective is not to convert all users into active contributors, but to encourage gradual movement up the ladder, increasing both the depth and value of engagement over time. Higher levels of engagement generate stronger emotional connections, greater brand visibility, and more credible peer-to-peer influence, making the framework particularly valuable for analysing social media strategies.

03 — Platform Evaluation

Instagram & The Four Zones of Social Media

Instagram Overview

Instagram is a visual-first social media platform founded in 2010, acquired by Meta in 2012, and has since grown to over two billion monthly active users globally (Statista, 2023). Originally a photo-sharing application, the platform has evolved to incorporate Reels, Stories, live streaming, and shoppable posts, positioning it as a comprehensive brand communication tool across multiple consumer touchpoints (Sheldon and Newman, 2019).

Figure 3. Instagram monthly user from Jan 2013 to Dec 2021 (Statista, 2023)

Instagram's suitability for the Ladder of Engagement framework stems from its inherently interactive and participatory design. Unlike traditional broadcast media, Instagram facilitates bidirectional communication between brands and consumers, with each native feature mapping directly onto a distinct level of the engagement ladder. Its emphasis on visual storytelling, community building, and peer-to-peer sharing makes it particularly effective for cultivating authentic, long-term consumer relationships rather than purely transactional interactions (Tuten and Solomon, 2020). For these reasons, Instagram represents an ideal platform through which to examine the Ladder of Engagement in practice.

Evaluation Using the Four Zones of Social Media and the Ladder of Engagement

Instagram operates across all Four Zones of Social Media identified by Tuten and Solomon (2020), functioning simultaneously as a space for social community, social publishing, social entertainment, and social commerce. This multi-zone presence makes Instagram uniquely suited for brands such as Toyota Gazoo Racing (TGR), enabling comprehensive consumer engagement across distinct digital environments. When examined alongside the Ladder of Engagement (Chaffey and Smith, 2022), it becomes evident that each zone facilitates a progressively deeper level of consumer participation, moving users from passive consumption toward active co-creation.

Figure 4. The Zones of social media illustration (Tuten, 2013)

Within the social community zone, Instagram fosters two-way dialogue through comments, direct messages, and interactive Story features such as polls and question stickers. Kent and Taylor (1998) argue that genuine dialogic communication requires platforms to enable responsiveness and reciprocity, both of which Instagram's architecture directly supports. TGR leverages this through community-focused content that invites fans to respond, share opinions, and participate in conversations surrounding motorsport events, thereby elevating users from the Consumption level toward the Conversation level of the engagement ladder.

The social publishing zone is reflected in Instagram's capacity for brand-controlled content dissemination through Feed posts, Reels, and Stories. Dolan et al. (2019) distinguish between active and passive engagement behaviours, noting that passive consumption of published content still contributes to brand awareness and recall. For TGR, visually compelling race footage and behind-the-scenes Reels serve as entry points for new audiences, capturing users at the foundational Consumption rung before nurturing them upward through consistent, high-quality publishing.

The social entertainment zone is perhaps where Instagram holds its strongest advantage for TGR. Reels and live streaming transform brand content into immersive, participatory experiences. Sheldon and Newman (2019) highlight entertainment and escapism as primary motivations for Instagram use, suggesting that brands delivering entertaining content are more likely to sustain user attention and emotional investment. TGR's race-day live streams and driver-focused storytelling satisfy these motivational drivers, encouraging users to move from Interaction toward Advocacy as emotional connections deepen.

Finally, Instagram's social commerce zone, facilitated through shoppable posts and product tags, enables TGR to convert engaged followers into transactional participants. While co-creation at the top of the engagement ladder is largely non-commercial in nature, commerce features bridge the gap between community engagement and brand loyalty, reinforcing the overall consumer relationship.

Collectively, these frameworks demonstrate that Instagram is not a singular engagement tool but rather a layered ecosystem in which different features serve distinct strategic purposes. TGR's presence across all four zones ensures that users at every level of the engagement ladder are catered for, from the casual viewer encountering a Reel for the first time to the dedicated fan co-creating content and advocating for the brand organically. This alignment between platform architecture, consumer motivation, and brand strategy reinforces Instagram as an optimal channel for TGR's digital marketing objectives.

Justification

The fifteen criteria selected for this checklist were drawn from a combination of Nielsen's (2006) usability heuristics, Chaffey and Smith's (2022) RACE planning framework, and Kent and Taylor's (1998) dialogic communication principles, ensuring a comprehensive and academically grounded evaluation of the TGR website.

Criteria 1 and 2 address technical performance and mobile responsiveness, which Chaffey and Smith (2022) identify as foundational requirements for any effective digital presence, particularly as mobile browsing now accounts for over half of global web traffic. TGR scores highly in both areas, reflecting its investment in technical infrastructure.

Criteria 3 and 4 examine navigation and visual content quality. Nielsen (2006) emphasises that intuitive navigation is critical to reducing user frustration and improving dwell time. TGR's website features a well-structured menu system and striking motorsport imagery, reinforcing its premium brand positioning, though navigation could be further streamlined for new visitors.

Criteria 5 and 13 evaluate calls-to-action (CTAs) and customer engagement features respectively. Both received a neutral rating, as whilst TGR provides links to race schedules and vehicle information, the website lacks interactive features such as live chat, quizzes, or community forums that would elevate engagement beyond passive consumption, as advocated by Dolan et al. (2019).

Criteria 6 and 7 assess social media integration and content currency. TGR performs strongly here, embedding Instagram and YouTube feeds directly into the site and regularly updating race results and news, aligning with Chaffey and Smith's (2022) recommendation for cross-channel consistency.

Criteria 8 and 11 cover SEO optimisation and privacy policy accessibility. TGR demonstrates strong search visibility through structured metadata and keyword-rich content, whilst its privacy policy is clearly accessible from the footer, meeting GDPR compliance standards.

Criteria 9 and 15 evaluate brand identity and value communication. TGR achieves the highest rating in both, consistently projecting its core values of performance, innovation, and motorsport heritage across all pages, demonstrating effective brand storytelling as outlined by Tuten and Solomon (2020).

Criteria 10 and 14 revealed areas for improvement. Contact information is not immediately visible on the homepage, and accessibility features for users with disabilities, such as screen reader compatibility and alt text consistency, remain inconsistent, representing notable gaps against modern digital inclusion standards.

Finally, Criterion 12 highlights TGR's strength in multilingual support, offering content across numerous languages, which is essential for a globally operating motorsport brand competing across multiple international markets.

05 — Trend Analysis

User-Generated Content in Motorsport Marketing

Overview: User-Generated Content in Motorsport Marketing

User-Generated Content (UGC) has emerged as one of the most significant digital marketing trends of the last five years. Defined by Tuten and Solomon (2020, p.4) as "any content created and shared by consumers rather than brands", UGC has fundamentally shifted brand communication from one-way broadcasting to participatory, community-driven ecosystems. This shift has been accelerated by the proliferation of short-form video platforms such as TikTok and Instagram Reels, which lowered the barrier to content creation for everyday consumers (Chaffey and Smith, 2022). Christodoulides, Jevons and Bonhomme (2012) provide quantitative evidence that UGC significantly influences consumer purchasing decisions, highlighting the commercial significance of UGC beyond mere engagement metrics. Within motorsport, UGC has proven particularly powerful due to the highly passionate nature of motorsport fanbases, generating organic reach that far exceeds equivalent paid advertising (Dolan et al., 2019).

TGR's Participation: #GazooRacing, #GRgram and GR Garage

Toyota Gazoo Racing has been an active and documented participant in this trend, most notably through the #GazooRacing and #GRgram hashtag campaigns and its GR Garage owner community platform, all developed consistently between 2020 and 2024.

The #GazooRacing hashtag has accumulated over 400,000 posts on Instagram (Instagram, 2024a), whilst the complementary #GRgram hashtag, officially encouraged by TGR to unite GR vehicle owners and enthusiasts, has contributed an additional 68,000 posts (Instagram, 2024b), collectively demonstrating the scale of TGR's UGC ecosystem across the platform.

TGR actively reshares fan content across its official channels, co-creating its brand narrative with its community (Tuten and Solomon, 2020), which directly reflects the co-creation level of Chaffey and Smith's (2022) Ladder of Engagement.

According to Statista (2023), Instagram alone hosts over 500 million daily active Stories users, demonstrating the significant scale of the platform through which TGR's UGC ecosystem operates.

The GR Garage initiative further demonstrates Kent and Taylor's (1998) principle of dialogic communication, providing a physical and digital community space where GR vehicle owners share driving experiences and brand stories, creating genuine two-way brand relationships. Additionally, during the 2021 and 2022 WRC seasons, TGR encouraged fans to share content using official hashtags, which was aggregated and featured across its digital platforms, leveraging Cialdini's (2001) principle of social proof to amplify brand desirability to wider audiences. This strategy aligns with findings by Kemp (2023), who reported that 4.9 billion people globally were active social media users in 2023, representing an unprecedented audience pool for UGC-driven campaigns to reach organically.

Critical Evaluation

Whilst TGR's UGC strategy demonstrates considerable strengths, limitations remain. Nielsen's (2006) 90-9-1 rule suggests that 90% of users consume content, 9% contribute occasionally, and only 1% actively create, meaning TGR must continuously incentivise participation to sustain campaign momentum beyond its core advocate base. This is further supported by Dolan et al. (2019), who distinguish between active engagement behaviours such as creating and sharing, and passive engagement such as viewing and liking, noting that the majority of users remain passive even within highly active communities. Furthermore, UGC presents brand control challenges, as independently created content cannot guarantee message consistency, potentially exposing TGR to reputational risks if off-brand content gains visibility under official hashtags (Tuten and Solomon, 2020).

Nevertheless, by positioning its community as co-creators rather than passive consumers, TGR has successfully cultivated a digitally engaged global fanbase that generates sustained organic reach and emotional brand loyalty that traditional advertising cannot replicate, reflecting a sophisticated and academically grounded approach to contemporary digital marketing.

06 — Conclusion

Synthesis and Critical Reflection

This e-portfolio has critically examined Instagram as a digital marketing platform and evaluated Toyota Gazoo Racing's (TGR) strategic use of social media between 2020 and 2024. Through the application of established academic frameworks including Tuten and Solomon's (2020) Four Zones of Social Media, Chaffey and Smith's (2022) Ladder of Engagement, Kent and Taylor's (1998) dialogic communication principles, and Cialdini's (2001) social proof theory, it has been demonstrated that TGR operates a sophisticated, multi-dimensional digital marketing strategy that extends significantly beyond conventional promotional broadcasting.

The website evaluation conducted in Section 4 revealed that whilst TGR's digital presence is largely effective, opportunities remain to strengthen two-way communication and accessibility features, reflecting Nielsen's (2006) usability principles. Section 5 demonstrated that TGR's UGC strategy, anchored by the #GazooRacing and #GRgram campaigns and the GR Garage community platform, successfully positions its fanbase as active co-creators rather than passive consumers, generating sustained organic reach that conventional paid advertising cannot replicate.

However, critical limitations were identified throughout. Nielsen's (2006) 90-9-1 rule highlights the persistent challenge of converting passive audiences into active contributors, whilst Dolan et al. (2019) remind us that the majority of social media users remain behaviorally passive despite apparent community activity. Furthermore, UGC campaigns inherently carry brand control risks, as independently created content cannot always guarantee message consistency (Tuten and Solomon, 2020).

Looking ahead, the continued growth of short-form video content, AI-driven personalisation, and social commerce features within Instagram will present both opportunities and challenges for brands like TGR. To maintain competitive advantage, TGR should prioritise deeper community incentivisation, expanded dialogic communication channels, and robust UGC moderation strategies to ensure brand integrity whilst sustaining authentic community engagement.

In conclusion, TGR's digital marketing approach reflects a genuine understanding of contemporary consumer behaviour and platform dynamics. When evaluated against the academic frameworks applied throughout this portfolio, TGR demonstrates best practice in several key areas, making it a compelling case study in motorsport digital marketing. Nevertheless, continued critical evaluation and strategic adaptation will remain essential as the digital landscape evolves.
