Section 1 — Introduction

Portfolio overview, objectives and structure

Portfolio Overview

This e-portfolio has been produced as part of the MKT 3039 Digital Marketing module at the University of Northampton. It presents a structured critical analysis of the digital marketing strategies employed by Toyota Gazoo Racing (TGR), one of the most recognisable motorsport and performance vehicle sub-brands in the global automotive industry.

The portfolio examines TGR's presence across social media, with a primary focus on Instagram, as well as evaluating the organisation's official website against established usability and engagement frameworks. A forward-looking trend analysis is also included, examining the rise of user-generated content and community-driven campaigns within the automotive digital marketing landscape from 2020 to 2024.

Objectives

  • To critically evaluate TGR's Instagram strategy using Tuten and Solomon's (2020) Four Zones of Social Media
  • To assess audience engagement using Chaffey and Smith's (2022) Ladder of Engagement
  • To evaluate TGR's official website using a 15-item Likert scale checklist
  • To analyse the trend of UGC and community-driven campaigns between 2020 and 2024
  • To provide evidence-based recommendations for future digital marketing improvements

Portfolio Structure

Section 2 — Instagram Overview

Platform history, growth and the Ladder of Engagement

Platform History

Instagram was founded in 2010 by Kevin Systrom and Mike Krieger as a photo-sharing application. Its rapid growth attracted the attention of Meta (then Facebook), which acquired the platform in 2012 for approximately $1 billion. Since its acquisition, Instagram has evolved significantly, introducing features such as Stories (2016), IGTV (2018), Reels (2020) and Shopping integrations, transforming it from a simple photo app into a comprehensive social commerce and content platform.

The Ladder of Engagement

Chaffey and Smith (2022) propose the Ladder of Engagement as a framework for understanding progressive levels of audience interaction with brand content on social media platforms. The model describes five ascending levels of engagement:

  • Consumption — Users passively view content without interaction
  • Curation — Users save or bookmark content for later reference
  • Creation — Users generate original content related to the brand
  • Collaboration — Users actively co-create content with the brand
  • Co-creation — Users become brand advocates, shaping brand narratives

Within the Instagram context, this framework is particularly relevant as the platform's algorithm rewards higher engagement levels, amplifying content that generates saves, shares and comments over passive likes alone (Sheldon and Newman, 2019).

Instagram as an Automotive Marketing Platform

The automotive sector has increasingly leveraged Instagram's visual-first format to communicate brand identity, showcase vehicle aesthetics and build enthusiast communities. Stronks (2021) notes that motorsport brands in particular benefit from Instagram's capacity for high-impact imagery and real-time event coverage, enabling brands like TGR to maintain relevance between racing seasons through consistent content delivery.

Section 3 — TGR Social Media Evaluation

Four Zones framework and Ladder of Engagement analysis

Tuten and Solomon's Four Zones of Social Media

Tuten and Solomon (2020) classify social media activity into four distinct zones, each representing a different type of interaction between brands and audiences. This framework provides a structured lens through which TGR's Instagram presence can be systematically evaluated.

Ladder of Engagement — TGR Analysis

Applying Chaffey and Smith's (2022) Ladder of Engagement to TGR's Instagram account reveals a predominantly mid-tier engagement profile. The majority of TGR's 9.3 million followers engage at the Consumption and Curation levels, demonstrated by high view counts on Reels and strong save rates on vehicle showcase posts.

Key Finding: TGR's #GazooRacing hashtag has accumulated over 400,000 posts, indicating a significant proportion of followers have progressed to the Creation and Co-creation levels of the Ladder of Engagement, producing and sharing their own GR-themed content organically.

However, evidence of structured Collaboration remains limited. TGR does not currently operate a formal brand ambassador or co-creation programme on Instagram, meaning the progression from Creation to Collaboration relies primarily on organic community behaviour rather than strategic facilitation.

Section 4 — Website Evaluation

15-item Likert scale checklist and justification

Evaluation Framework

The TGR website was evaluated using a 15-item Likert scale checklist developed from three established frameworks: Nielsen's (2006) usability heuristics, Kent and Taylor's (1998) dialogic communication principles, and Dolan et al.'s (2019) social media engagement criteria. Each item was scored on a scale of 1 (Strongly Disagree) to 5 (Strongly Agree).

Justification

The checklist draws on three complementary theoretical frameworks to provide a holistic evaluation of the TGR website. Nielsen's (2006) usability heuristics form the foundation of criteria 1–5, assessing the technical and navigational performance of the site. The TGR website scores consistently well across these metrics, reflecting Toyota's significant investment in digital infrastructure.

Kent and Taylor's (1998) dialogic communication principles underpin criteria 6–10, evaluating the extent to which the website facilitates genuine two-way communication between brand and audience. Here, TGR's performance is markedly weaker, particularly in relation to user feedback mechanisms (criteria 7 and 8), where the absence of visible feedback channels represents a missed opportunity for relationship building.

Dolan et al.'s (2019) engagement framework informs criteria 11–15, assessing the website's capacity to stimulate active audience participation. The most critical weakness identified is criterion 13, where the complete absence of UGC showcasing on the website represents a significant disconnect from TGR's active Instagram community and its thriving #GazooRacing hashtag ecosystem.

Section 5 — Trend Analysis

UGC and community-driven campaigns 2020–2024

Trend Overview: User-Generated Content

Between 2020 and 2024, user-generated content (UGC) emerged as one of the most significant trends in digital marketing, fundamentally reshaping how brands communicate authenticity and build community. Christodoulides et al. (2012) define UGC as any form of content created and publicly shared by unpaid contributors, noting its capacity to carry greater perceived credibility than brand-produced content.

Within the automotive sector, this trend has been amplified by the growth of enthusiast communities on Instagram, where owner-generated content — from modification showcases to track day footage — has created organic marketing ecosystems that brands can leverage at minimal cost.

TGR's #GazooRacing hashtag campaign represents one of the most successful automotive UGC initiatives on Instagram. With over 400,000 posts accumulated between 2020 and 2024, the hashtag has become a centralised community hub through which GR vehicle owners, motorsport fans and racing enthusiasts share content organically. This aligns with Chaffey and Smith's (2022) Co-creation level of the Ladder of Engagement, where audiences actively contribute to brand narrative construction.

Cialdini's Social Proof (2001): The volume of #GazooRacing posts functions as a powerful social proof mechanism, signalling to prospective customers that GR vehicles are aspirational, community-endorsed products. When potential buyers encounter hundreds of thousands of enthusiast posts, the perceived desirability of the brand is reinforced through peer validation rather than paid advertising.

The secondary hashtag #GRgram, with 68,000 posts, serves a more curated function, primarily used by owners to showcase vehicle aesthetics and modifications. This distinction is significant: while #GazooRacing captures broad motorsport engagement, #GRgram represents a deeper owner-identity community, where users express personal investment in the GR brand ecosystem.

Nielsen's 90-9-1 Rule Applied to TGR

Nielsen's (2006) 90-9-1 rule proposes that in any online community, 90% of participants consume content passively, 9% contribute occasionally, and only 1% create the majority of content. Applied to TGR's Instagram community of 9.3 million followers, this model suggests:

  • ~8.37 million followers — Passive consumers of TGR content
  • ~837,000 followers — Occasional engagers (likes, saves, story interactions)
  • ~93,000 followers — Active content creators contributing to #GazooRacing and #GRgram

The 400,000 #GazooRacing posts therefore represent the cumulative output of this active 1% tier over four years, underscoring both the loyalty and creative investment of TGR's core community. However, Nielsen's model also highlights the untapped potential within the 9% occasional engagement tier, which represents a significant opportunity for TGR to convert moderate engagers into active content contributors through structured incentivisation.

Section 6 — Conclusion

Key findings, limitations and future recommendations

Key Findings

This portfolio has demonstrated that Toyota Gazoo Racing operates a sophisticated and visually compelling digital marketing strategy across Instagram, underpinned by high-quality content production and an organically thriving UGC community. The application of Tuten and Solomon's (2020) Four Zones framework revealed particular strength in the Social Publishing and Social Entertainment zones, while Social Commerce remains an underdeveloped dimension of TGR's Instagram presence.

The website evaluation identified strong usability performance in line with Nielsen's (2006) heuristics, but significant weaknesses in dialogic communication (Kent and Taylor, 1998) and UGC integration (Dolan et al., 2019). The absence of user feedback mechanisms and the failure to showcase community-generated content on the website represents a missed opportunity to bridge TGR's Instagram community and its owned digital channels.

The trend analysis confirmed that TGR's hashtag campaigns (#GazooRacing and #GRgram) have successfully leveraged UGC as a community-building mechanism, generating social proof effects consistent with Cialdini's (2001) framework. However, the application of Nielsen's (2006) 90-9-1 rule highlights that the majority of TGR's audience remains at the passive consumption level, representing a strategic gap in community activation.

Recommendations

  • Formalise UGC Incentivisation — Introduce a structured brand ambassador or community rewards programme to convert the 9% occasional engagement tier into active content contributors
  • Integrate UGC into Website — Embed a live #GazooRacing feed or curated gallery on the TGR website to bridge the gap between social community and owned digital channels
  • Strengthen Dialogic Communication — Implement visible user feedback mechanisms on the website, including review sections, community forums and driver story submissions
  • Expand Social Commerce — Develop a more comprehensive Instagram Shopping strategy, including product tagging on GR merchandise and direct links to the vehicle configurator tool
  • Implement UGC Moderation Policy — Develop transparent community guidelines and a moderation framework to maintain brand safety as UGC volume continues to scale

Limitations

This analysis is subject to several limitations. Hashtag post counts were recorded at a single point in time and are subject to continuous change. The website evaluation reflects the author's subjective application of the Likert scale, and scores may vary between evaluators. Additionally, Instagram engagement data beyond publicly visible metrics (such as reach and impression data) was not accessible, limiting the depth of quantitative analysis possible within this portfolio.
